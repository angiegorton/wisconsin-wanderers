Welcome to the Sheboygan Falls Asylum

(Cat Toys, Fireball, and Spirit Boxes, Oh My!)

Okay so in theory, exploring an abandoned asylum sounds really freaking cool. One of the highest levels of badassery. And in some ways it is. But here’s my counter offer: I’m scared of trespassing. Not trespassing itself but getting caught, and fined, and getting a citation blah blah blah. So what are our loopholes here? How do I get in the abandoned Sheboygan Falls Asylum legally? Answer: sign up for a guided ghost tour of the place at night. And that’s exactly what I did after roping a fellow wanderer to go with me on a mini ghost road trip. Lemme tell you, walking through that asylum in the dark was really trippy. But the people that I was on tour with? Crazier than anything I saw that night.


Let me preface this by saying that I’m not one to really believe in the supernatural, I just think going to creepy sites and exploring is fun. So no, I don’t think I felt any ghostly presence or saw any specters in the air. However, the tour guides and the people on tour were paranormal in it of themselves. They wholeheartedly believed that they were talking to ghosts. Which I won’t judge them for. But let me run you through a list of reasons why I think this ghost tour was absolutely not haunted and why the people on this tour were kinda, maybe, very delusional.





Their Ghost Hunting Tools
Two words. Cat. Toys. Cat Toys. Let me say that one more time to hammer it home. Cat toys. The guides would throw cat toys down a hallway. If they lit up, then it had to be because of a spiritual presence. Definitely not because the technology behind cat toys is highly archaic and sensitive to any movement. We would genuinely stand in the dark and stare in silence to see if the cat toys would light up due to a “ghost” entering the room. Bizarre, just bizarre.
Fireball
A beverage every college kid is well acquainted with. I’ll bet you’ll never guess who else is well acquainted with Fireball. The ghosts of Sheboygan Falls Asylum (allegedly). Imagine my surprise when my group is touring the boiler room of the asylum and I see a mini shrine of Fireball shooters on the ground. As the observant person I am, I ask what those are. To which the guide tells me that the “ghosts of old maintenance workers” informed her that they wanted Fireball, and so they made a Fireball shrine. Let’s do a mini history lesson. This asylum was open from 1876 to 1940. Fireball wasn’t popular in the US until the mid-2000s. I’m just going to say it out loud. The “ghosts” do not want Fireball. They had no idea what Fireball was, they were before its time. Unless the “ghosts” are susceptible to advertising and peer pressure, I think the group was under the influence of a hefty dose of delusion, not Fireball.
The Spirit Box
That ole beaut was allegedly how the tour guides knew the ghosts wanted Fireball. For those of you unfamiliar with spirit boxes, let me explain it to you based on what the tour guides told me when I asked. The Spirit Box flips through a bunch of AM radio stations (since it was night and there were less people active on AM stations) very quickly. So normally you won’t hear words or phrases strung together - unless a spirit is trying to make contact with you. Okay, cool, makes sense if you maybe believe in that type of thing. So then the guides and fellow people on tour would ask the spirit box questions and wait for the radio to reveal some answers from beyond the grave. Let me give you an example of how this dialgue would usually roll out.

Hello. Can you tell us how many of you are in the room with us?
Dfkjghdkghwkf

3? There are 3 presences in the room?
Dsfhsdkjfhskdjfhadhof

Okay. Can you tell me your names?
Kdjfhdkhfksdnf

I think I heard the name Dave. Is that correct?
Sedjkfnwkejfwgj jfkwedhsfkewj

Notice anything? Let me give it to you straight. The spirit box is a potent shot of placebo. Maybe half-formed words or almost-phrases would come from the spirit box occasionally, but that makes sense since its stringing together vowels and different consonants from the radio station. The responses from the spirit box to the above questions produced exactly what you saw. Unintelligible garbage. The people on tour were listening to the box so hard and wanting to hear something so bad, their ears started playing tricks on them. No, the “ghost” didn’t say their name was Dave, and no there were not 3 other “ghosts” in the room. All there was were random muffled sounds emitted from a technological waste of time and a group of people desperate to experience something. I rest my case.
Conclusion
Do I regret going? No, of course not. The abandoned asylum was a great place to explore, especially at night. Do I regret going with the ghost tour ? Again, no. They were honestly really entertaining and more ominous than anything I saw in the corridors of the asylum. Wherever my tour group is right now, I hope they’re having fun with their cat toys, Fireball, and spirit boxes.
